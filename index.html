Literature Review

3.1
Prediction Markets
Well-Calibrated Forecasts
  • Across 5 elections from 1988 to 2004, Iowa Electronic Markets beat 964 polls 74% of the time, performing even better for forecasts up to 100 days out (Berg et al., 2008)
  • Prediction market prices respond rapidly to new information, reinforcing their success as real-time probability measures that aggregate dispersed beliefs
Correlated Prices Reveal Connected Markets
  • Leigh et al. (2003) found that a 10% increase in the probability of war raised oil prices approximately $1 per barrel and lowered equity prices by about 1.5%
  • Observing how S&P futures move with election odds allows analysts to reveal the implied market move given a particular candidate wins (Snowberg et al., 2007)
Favorite-Longshot Bias
  • High-likelihood events tend to be underpriced while low-likelihood events are overpriced, and the effect diminishes as contracts approach expiration (Page & Clemen, 2012)
  • This bias can be argued to partially result from interest rates giving the favorite a higher opportunity cost of capital, making long-dated favorite contracts less attractive to hold
3.2
Traditional Volatility Modeling
ARCH
Engle, 1982
  • Before Engle, econometric models assumed constant volatility when modeling assets
  • ARCH allows conditional variance to depend on past squared errors, capturing volatility clustering (calm periods followed by turbulent ones)
  • The model restricts the coefficient on past volatility to be less than one to prevent variance from growing without bound
→
GARCH
Bollerslev, 1986
  • ARCH required many time lags and did not incorporate the past level of conditional variance itself
  • GARCH adds a lagged variance term so today's expected volatility depends on both past shocks and the previous level of expected volatility
  • In effect, GARCH adds a smoothing component to the ARCH framework
→
Stochastic Volatility
Nelson, 1990 · Heston, 1993
  • Nelson showed that as time intervals shrink, ARCH and GARCH converge to stochastic differential equations — bridging discrete and continuous time
  • Heston used Itô calculus to build the first closed-form stochastic volatility model, closing the gap between Black-Scholes and deterministic variance
  • Established SDEs as the natural framework for modeling uncertainty that changes with time

Modeling Volatility in Prediction Markets

Archak & Ipeirotis (2008) derived a volatility expression that depends only on the current contract price and time to expiration, not past returns. Their model outperforms GARCH for prediction markets, but assumes pure diffusion with no jumps. They explain the relationship between volatility, time, and price.

Generalized Contract Price

Assumptions

About the Difference Process S(t)
1. Correlated diffusions. The Brownian motions W_1(t) and W_2(t) have constant correlation \rho_{12} \in (-1,1).
2. Independent jumps. The compound Poisson processes J_1(t) and J_2(t) are independent of each other and of the Brownian motions.
3. Constant coefficients. All parameters \mu_i, \sigma_i, \lambda_i, \mu_{J_i}, \sigma_{J_i} are constant over the life of the contract.
4. Markov property. The contract price depends only on the current difference: \pi(t) = \mathbb{P}(S(T) > 0 \mid S(t)).

Terminal Ability Difference

Integral Representation
S(T) = S(t) + (\mu_1 - \mu_2)\,\tau + \sigma\bigl(W(T) - W(t)\bigr) + \sum_{k=1}^{N_1(\tau)} Y_{1,k} - \sum_{j=1}^{N_2(\tau)} Y_{2,j}
Compensated Form
S(T) = S(t) + \mu\tau + \sigma\bigl(W(T) - W(t)\bigr) + \bigl(\tilde{J}(T) - \tilde{J}(t)\bigr)
\mu = (\mu_1 - \mu_2) + (\lambda_1 \mu_{J_1} - \lambda_2 \mu_{J_2}) — net drift, combining baseline diffusion drifts and expected jump contributions \tau = T - t — time remaining until expiration \sigma = \sqrt{\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 - 2\rho_{12}\,\sigma_1 \sigma_2} — combined diffusion coefficient from the two correlated Brownian motions N_i(\tau) \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda_i \tau) — number of jumps for party i in [t, T], independent by assumption 2 Y_{1,k} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_{J_1}, \sigma_{J_1}^2) and Y_{2,j} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_{J_2}, \sigma_{J_2}^2) — independent jump sizes for each party

The Problem: A Non-Gaussian Distribution

Ideally, we could simply find the mean and variance of S(T) \mid \mathcal{F}_t, standardize, and arrive at a closed-form price. But this assumes S(T) is normally distributed — exactly the simplification our framework is meant to move beyond.

Why it breaks down:

  • The number of Poisson shocks between t and T is random. More shocks increase variance and shift the mean, since each jump carries its own expected drift.
  • Each possible jump count yields a different Gaussian conditional distribution. The unconditional distribution is a mixture of these Gaussians, and a mixture of normals is not itself normal.
  • The result is a fat-tailed distribution: extreme outcomes are far more likely than a single Gaussian would predict, reflecting the heavy tails we observed in real market data.

The Solution: Conditioning on Jumps

We resolve this by conditioning on a fixed number of jumps. If we know exactly how many jumps occur, the only remaining randomness comes from Brownian motion — which is Gaussian. This gives us a tractable conditional distribution:

Conditional Distribution
S(T) \;\Big|\; \mathcal{F}_t,\, N_1 = n_1,\, N_2 = n_2 \;\;\sim\;\; \mathcal{N}\!\left(m_{n_1,n_2},\; v_{n_1,n_2}\right)
m_{n_1,n_2} = S(t) + (\mu_1 - \mu_2)\,\tau + n_1\,\mu_{J_1} - n_2\,\mu_{J_2} — conditional mean v_{n_1,n_2} = \sigma^2 \tau + n_1\,\sigma_{J_1}^2 + n_2\,\sigma_{J_2}^2 — conditional variance

We then sum over all possible jump counts, weighted by their Poisson probabilities. The result is the generalized contract price:

Generalized Contract Price
\pi(t) \;=\; \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} p(n;\,\lambda\tau)\,\Phi(Z_n)
p(n;\,\lambda\tau) = \dfrac{e^{-\lambda\tau}(\lambda\tau)^n}{n!} — Poisson PMF: probability of exactly n jumps in [t, T] Z_n = \dfrac{S(t) + (\mu_1 - \mu_2)\,\tau + n\,\mu_J}{\sqrt{\sigma^2\tau + n\,\sigma_J^2}} — standardized score given n jumps
Each term is the probability of exactly n jumps times the price that would prevail if n jumps were guaranteed. The Poisson weights sum to one.
N_1 2
N_2 1
Remark (Convergence)

The infinite sum converges rapidly since p(n;\,\lambda\tau) has n! in its denominator, which grows much faster than the numerator. We truncate at

N_{\max} = \lceil\lambda\tau\rceil + 4\sqrt{\lambda\tau}

covering four standard deviations from the Poisson mean, over 99.99% of the distribution's mass.

Generalized Price Sensitivities

Partial Derivatives of Price

To derive instantaneous volatility via Ito's formula, we need the partial derivatives of \pi(t, S) with respect to S and t. Since \pi(t) is an infinite sum, we differentiate term by term using the known relationships \frac{d\Phi}{dx}(x) = \phi(x) and \frac{d\phi}{dx}(x) = -x\,\phi(x).

First-Order: w.r.t. Ability
\frac{\partial\pi}{\partial S} \;=\; \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} p(n;\,\lambda\tau) \cdot \frac{\phi(Z_n)}{\sqrt{v_n}}
  • \phi(Z_n) peaks when the race is closest (Z_n = 0), while it decays for large |Z_n|.
  • Inversely proportional to \sqrt{v_n}: more jumps, higher \sigma, or longer \tau dampens sensitivity.
  • A trader cares less about small ability changes when substantial time or variance remains.
Second-Order: w.r.t. Ability
\frac{\partial^2\pi}{\partial S^2} \;=\; \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} p(n;\,\lambda\tau) \cdot \frac{-Z_n\,\phi(Z_n)}{v_n}
  • The additional factor of -Z_n means that when one party is already far ahead (large |Z_n|), further changes in ability have a diminishing effect on price.
  • Convexity is highest near Z_n = 0.
First-Order: w.r.t. Time
  • \partial\pi / \partial t is computable via the product rule since both Poisson weights and Z_n depend on \tau.
  • Not needed: we show the drift of \pi(t) is zero.

Martingale Property & Ito Expansion

The Martingale Property of Price

Martingale Property
Under our assumptions and by the tower property (law of iterated expectations), the contract price \pi(t) = \mathbb{P}(S(T) > 0 \mid \mathcal{F}_t) is a martingale:
\mathbb{E}[\pi(t + dt) \mid \mathcal{F}_t] \;=\; \pi(t)
  • The expected future price, given all current information, equals today's price — no predictable drift.
  • This is a direct consequence of market efficiency: the current price already incorporates all available information.
  • If price were expected to drift, traders would buy or sell until it reached the expected level, eliminating the drift.

Ito Expansion of Price

Full Ito Formula for Jump-Diffusion
Since \pi(t) is a martingale, the Ito expansion of d\pi contains no drift term. Applying Ito's formula for jump-diffusion processes:
d\pi \;=\; \frac{\partial\pi}{\partial t}\,dt + \frac{\partial\pi}{\partial S}\,dS^c + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2\pi}{\partial S^2}(dS^c)^2 + \sum_{\text{jumps}}\!\bigl[\pi(t,\,S + \Delta\tilde{J}) - \pi(t,\,S)\bigr]
dS^c = \mu\,dt + \sigma\,dW is the continuous part of dS All drift terms (anything proportional to dt) cancel by the martingale property
Stochastic Components Only
After cancellation, only the stochastic components remain:
d\pi(t) \;=\; \sigma\,\frac{\partial\pi}{\partial S}\,dW(t) + \bigl[\text{jump-driven price changes}\bigr]
  • The first term captures continuous price fluctuations driven by Brownian motion, scaled by the first derivative \partial\pi / \partial S.
  • The second term captures discrete jumps: sudden repricing when a shock hits.
  • Zero drift means the average trader's beliefs about each party's parameters are correct — price reflects all prior information.

Generalized Instantaneous Volatility

The Full Expression

With the drift eliminated by the martingale property, the instantaneous variance of d\pi is composed of two parts: a continuous (diffusion) component and a jump component.

Generalized Instantaneous Volatility
\Sigma_{\mathrm{exact}}(t) = \sqrt{ \sigma^2\!\left(\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial S}\right)^{\!2} + \lambda_1 \,\mathbb{E}_{Y_1}\!\left[\bigl(\pi(S+Y_1)-\pi(S)\bigr)^2\right] + \lambda_2 \,\mathbb{E}_{Y_2}\!\left[\bigl(\pi(S-Y_2)-\pi(S)\bigr)^2\right] }
\displaystyle\frac{\partial\pi}{\partial S} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} p(n;\,\lambda\tau)\,\frac{\phi(Z_n)}{\sqrt{v_n}} is the first-order price sensitivity \mathbb{E}_{Y_i} averages over all possible jump sizes, weighted by Y_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_{J_i},\, \sigma_{J_i}^2)

What Each Term Captures

Continuous (Diffusion) Component
\sigma^2\!\left(\frac{\partial\pi}{\partial S}\right)^{\!2}
  • Price fluctuations driven by the continuous Brownian motion in the ability process.
  • Scaled by the price sensitivity \partial\pi/\partial S, how much the market reacts to small ability changes.
  • When \partial\pi/\partial S is large (tight race), even small diffusion moves the price substantially.
Jump Component
\lambda_i \,\mathbb{E}_{Y_i}\!\left[\bigl(\pi(S \pm Y_i) - \pi(S)\bigr)^2\right]
  • Additional price fluctuations caused by discrete, sudden shocks to each party's ability.
  • Weighted by jump intensity \lambda_i — more frequent jumps contribute more variance.
  • The squared price change (\pi(S \pm Y_i) - \pi(S))^2 is averaged over the full distribution of jump sizes.
\lambda 1.0
\sigma 0.8

Computation & Practical Considerations

Why No Closed-Form Solution
  • \Sigma_{\mathrm{exact}} has no closed-form solution — the jump terms require evaluating \pi(S + Y) for every possible jump size Y.
  • Although we truncate the Poisson sum so that both \pi(S+Y) and \partial\pi/\partial S are finite, we still must integrate over the distribution of Y.
  • Numerical integration (e.g., Gauss–Hermite quadrature) is required to evaluate the expectation \mathbb{E}_{Y_i}[\cdot].
  • Application also requires maximum likelihood estimation of all model parameters: \sigma,\, \mu_i,\, \lambda_i,\, \mu_{J_i},\, \sigma_{J_i}.
  • This makes computation considerably more expensive and involved — motivating the closed-form approximation we derive next.

Gaussian Approximation

Approximated Distribution

Expected Value & Variance
\mathbb{E}[S(T) \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = S(t) + \mu\,\tau
\mathrm{Var}[S(T) \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = \sigma^2\tau + \bigl[\lambda_1(\sigma_{J_1}^2 + \mu_{J_1}^2) + \lambda_2(\sigma_{J_2}^2 + \mu_{J_2}^2)\bigr]\tau = \sigma_{\mathrm{total}}^2\,\tau
\sigma_{\mathrm{total}}^2 = \sigma^2 + \lambda_1(\sigma_{J_1}^2 + \mu_{J_1}^2) + \lambda_2(\sigma_{J_2}^2 + \mu_{J_2}^2)
Approximate Distribution
S(T) \mid \mathcal{F}_t \;\dot{\sim}\; \mathcal{N}\!\left(S(t) + \mu\tau,\;\sigma_{\mathrm{total}}^2\tau\right)
  • When the expected number of jumps \lambda\tau is large, the Central Limit Theorem implies the Poisson mixture of Gaussians converges to a single Gaussian.
\lambda (normality) 0.0

Approximate Contract Price

Price
\pi(t) \;=\; \Phi(Z)
Standardized Score
Z \;=\; \frac{S(t) + \mu(T - t)}{\sigma_{\mathrm{total}}\sqrt{T - t}}
\mu = \mu_1 - \mu_2 + \lambda_1 \mu_{J_1} - \lambda_2 \mu_{J_2} — net drift \sigma = \sqrt{\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 - 2\rho_{12}\,\sigma_1 \sigma_2} — combined diffusion coefficient \sigma_{\mathrm{total}}^2 = \sigma^2 + \lambda_1(\sigma_{J_1}^2 + \mu_{J_1}^2) + \lambda_2(\sigma_{J_2}^2 + \mu_{J_2}^2) — total variance rate
Interpreting Z
  • Numerator S(t) + \mu(T-t): expected ability difference at expiration given current information.
  • Denominator \sigma_{\mathrm{total}}\sqrt{T-t}: total uncertainty remaining until expiration.
  • Z measures how far ahead party one is expected to be at time T, in units of uncertainty.
  • Price near $1 implies Z large and positive; price near $0.50 signals Z \approx 0.

Approximate Partial Derivatives

First-Order: w.r.t. Ability
\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial S} = \frac{\phi(Z)}{\sigma_{\mathrm{total}}\sqrt{T - t}}
  • Most sensitive when Z \approx 0 — parties evenly matched.
  • Inversely proportional to \sigma_{\mathrm{total}}\sqrt{T-t}: more uncertainty dampens impact.
  • When abilities are close, price reacts strongly to small changes.
Second-Order: w.r.t. Ability
\frac{\partial^2 \pi}{\partial S^2} = -\frac{Z\,\phi(Z)}{\sigma_{\mathrm{total}}^2(T - t)}
  • Diminishing marginal effect: same ability change has smaller impact when parties are further apart.
  • Changes in S have the greatest convexity effect when the race is tight.
First-Order: w.r.t. Time
\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial t} = \phi(Z) \cdot \frac{S(t) - \mu(T-t)}{2\,\sigma_{\mathrm{total}}(T-t)^{3/2}}
  • Changes in S have a greater impact when expiration is near — denominator shrinks as T-t \to 0.
  • Near expiration, even small ability differences cause large price swings.
Assumptions

1. Smoothness. The function \pi(t, S) = \Phi(Z(t,S)) is twice continuously differentiable (C^2) on the domain \{(t,S) : 0 \leq t < T,\; S \in \mathbb{R}\}.

2. Bounded jumps. There exists a constant M > 0 such that |\Delta \tilde{J}(t)| \leq M, where \Delta \tilde{J} denotes the compensated jump size.

3. Negligible higher-order terms. The bound M is sufficiently small that the third-order Taylor remainder satisfies |R_3| \leq \frac{M_3}{6}M^3 < 0.01, where M_3 = \sup_{|x - S| \leq M} |\partial^3 \pi / \partial S^3(t,x)|.

Zero Drift & Price Dynamics

Price Evolution (No Drift)
d\pi(t) \;=\; \sigma\,\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial S}\,dW(t) \;+\; \frac{\partial \pi}{\partial S}\,d\tilde{J}(t)
W(t) = (\sigma_1 W_1(t) - \sigma_2 W_2(t))/\sigma — combined Brownian motion \tilde{J}(t) — compensated jump process
Drift Cancellation
\left(\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial t} + \mu\,\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial S} + \frac{1}{2}\,\sigma_{\mathrm{total}}^2\,\frac{\partial^2 \pi}{\partial S^2}\right)dt \;=\; 0
  • The three drift components, time decay, expected ability change, and convexity, cancel exactly.
  • Price is a martingale: if it were expected to drift, traders would trade until the drift was eliminated.

Approximate Instantaneous Volatility

Closed-Form Volatility
\Sigma_{\mathrm{total}}(t) \;=\; \frac{\phi(Z)}{\sqrt{T - t}} \;=\; \frac{\phi\!\left(\Phi^{-1}(\pi(t))\right)}{\sqrt{T - t}}
Why This Is Interesting
  • This is the same expression Archak & Ipeirotis found for their original model. By relaxing their constraints to allow Poisson jumps, we still arrive at their formula under the Gaussian approximation.
  • All individual party parameters — \sigma_1, \sigma_2, \lambda_1, \lambda_2, \mu_{J_i}, \sigma_{J_i} — vanish from the volatility formula. Volatility depends only on the observable market price and time to expiration.
  • Price becomes more volatile as it moves toward $0.50 (tight race) and as time to expiration decreases.
  • No parameter estimation is needed — the two required inputs (\pi(t) and T-t) are directly observed in the market.

Results

Data Set Information

We empirically evaluate the model's volatility expression on historical data from Kalshi and Polymarket. We test across three market types that capture varying degrees of Brownian diffusion and Poisson shock contributions.

Data Set 1 of 3
Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC)
  • 12 Polymarket contracts on FOMC meeting decisions, from December 2024 to the currently active April 2026 market, measured at daily intervals.
  • Traders bet on rate decisions, and public discourse (the Brownian component) unfolds continuously through economic commentary and Fed-watching.
  • Shocks arrive discretely through scheduled data releases: jobs reports, CPI prints, and GDP revisions that abruptly shift rate expectations.
  • Why it fits our model: a clear blend of continuous information flow and discrete, impactful data shocks.
Data Set 2 of 3
Geopolitical Events
  • Pooled contracts on daily intervals from both Kalshi and Polymarket: Russia–Ukraine war, Iranian foreign affairs, Venezuelan presidency, Greenland acquisition, and the Strait of Hormuz closing.
  • Random shocks move these markets significantly more than the gradual flow of events, as the trading population is separated from the few decision-making officials.
  • Prices tend to remain stable until a major event (a ceasefire announcement, military escalation, or policy reversal) forces a sudden reassessment.
  • Why it fits our model: jump-dominated dynamics where Poisson shocks carry far more weight than the diffusion component.
Data Set 3 of 3
Premier League Soccer
  • Minute-level Kalshi data for nine "close games," defined as matches where the underdog becomes the favorite at least once during gameplay.
  • A density scan identifies the most actively traded 120-minute window; halftime (least active 15 minutes plus 5-minute buffers) is removed.
  • Shocks are less frequent (the Premier League averages only 0.28 penalty kicks per game, and injuries and red cards are rare), but when they occur, the impact on outcome is large.
  • Why it fits our model: ability-driven diffusion dominates, with infrequent but high-impact Poisson shocks (goals, penalties, red cards).

Empirical Validation of the Gaussian Approximation

Before assessing how well our volatility expressions predict observed volatility, we first verify that the Gaussian closed-form approximation closely matches the generalized expression derived in the exact proposition.

These results justify using the Gaussian closed-form \Sigma = \phi(Z)/\sqrt{\tau} in the regression analysis that follows. The approximation error is small, concentrated at short expiration horizons, and directionally predictable (slight underestimation). Given the significant computational cost of the generalized alternative, the Gaussian expression is the most practical choice — and as we show next, it performs remarkably well as a primary predictor for observed volatility.

Volatility Regression

We regress log absolute returns on the log of our model-implied volatility to test how accurately the closed-form expression explains observed volatility.

Regression Setup
Observed absolute returns decompose as |\Delta \pi_t| = h_t \cdot |\varepsilon_t|, where h_t is model-implied volatility and \varepsilon_t is i.i.d. noise.
Taking logs transforms this into an OLS-compatible form:
\log|\Delta \pi_t| = \log(h_t) + \log|\varepsilon_t|
The implied volatility is computed directly from price and time to expiration:
h_t = \frac{\phi(\Phi^{-1}(\pi(t)))}{\sqrt{T - t}}
If the theoretical expression accurately forecasts variance, we expect \beta = 1.

All three markets estimate \beta close to one, and \beta = 1 falls within every 95% confidence interval — we cannot reject the theoretical prediction in any market type.

Conclusion

What We Did
Extended the prediction market volatility framework by incorporating compound Poisson jumps into the latent ability processes.
Derived a generalized model that requires numerical integration — no closed-form solution exists.
Justified by the Central Limit Theorem, we derived a closed-form Gaussian approximation that turns out to be the same expression from the original framework, showing the original formula is robust to the presence of jumps.
Validated empirically across three distinct markets: FOMC rate decisions, geopolitical events, and Premier League soccer.
Found that the generalized and closed-form volatility expressions produce nearly identical forecasts, and the Gaussian approximation accurately characterizes observed volatility.
Open Questions & Future Work
The model assumes constant jump intensities and independent jump processes — relaxing either could improve fit in markets with regime changes.
The Gaussian approximation is least accurate near expiration. Future work could measure how much the model degrades as \tau \to 0, and whether this is more pronounced in markets that can expire at any time (e.g., ceasefire announcements).
The generalized model's price formula requires numerical integration and MLE parameter estimation, both computationally expensive at scale — more efficient estimation methods would make the generalized form practical for real-time applications.

Proof of Lemma: Integral Representation of S(T)

We begin by expanding the difference process S(t) = S_1(t) - S_2(t):

dS(t) = dS_1(t) - dS_2(t) = (\mu_1 dt + \sigma_1 dW_1 + dJ_1) - (\mu_2 dt + \sigma_2 dW_2 + dJ_2) = (\mu_1 - \mu_2) dt + (\sigma_1 dW_1 - \sigma_2 dW_2) + (dJ_1 - dJ_2)

The problem with the expression above is dJ_1 - dJ_2 is not a martingale, since the jump processes may have different nonzero expected drifts over time. We want the stochastic component of the model to be a martingale. If it were not, we would have some expectation about the direction of randomness. Therefore, we compensate the Poisson term by separating it into its expected drift, which may be nonzero if, for example, positive shocks are larger or more frequent than negative ones, and the remaining unpredictable variation. (See Appendix A for details):

d\tilde{J}_i = dJ_i - \lambda_i \mu_{J_i} dt, \quad \text{so that} \quad \mathbb{E}[d\tilde{J}_i] = 0

Substituting and collecting drift terms:

dS(t) = (\mu_1 - \mu_2 + \lambda_1\mu_{J_1} - \lambda_2\mu_{J_2}) dt + (\sigma_1 dW_1 - \sigma_2 dW_2) + (d\tilde{J}_1 - d\tilde{J}_2) = \mu dt + \sigma dW + d\tilde{J}

Where:

  • \mu = (\mu_1 - \mu_2 + \lambda_1\mu_{J_1} - \lambda_2\mu_{J_2})
  • d\tilde{J}(t) = d\tilde{J}_1 - d\tilde{J}_2
  • \sigma = \sqrt{\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 - 2\rho_{12}\sigma_1\sigma_2}
  • W = \frac{1}{\sigma}(\sigma_1 W_1 - \sigma_2 W_2)

Note: σ and W derivations shown in Appendix B.

Integrating from t to T. By the Markov property, \pi(t) = \mathbb{P}(S(T) > 0 \mid S(t)). Integrating:

S(T) = S(t) + \int_t^T \mu dt + \int_t^T \sigma dW + \int_t^T d\tilde{J} = S(t) + \mu\tau + \sigma(W(T)-W(t)) + (\tilde{J}(T)-\tilde{J}(t))

where \tau = T - t. We now expand the compensated jump increment. By definition:

\tilde{J}_i(T) - \tilde{J}_i(t) = \sum_{k=1}^{N_i(\tau)} Y_{i,k} - \lambda_i\mu_{J_i}\tau

where N_i(\tau) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_i\tau) counts the number of party-i jumps in [t,T] and Y_{i,k} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_{J_i}, \sigma_{J_i}^2) are i.i.d. jump sizes. Since \tilde{J} = \tilde{J}_1 - \tilde{J}_2:

\tilde{J}(T) - \tilde{J}(t) = \left(\sum_{k=1}^{N_1(\tau)} Y_{1,k} - \lambda_1\mu_{J_1}\tau\right) - \left(\sum_{j=1}^{N_2(\tau)} Y_{2,j} - \lambda_2\mu_{J_2}\tau\right)

Substituting into S(T), the \lambda_1\mu_{J_1}\tau terms from drift and compensation cancel. This leaves:

S(T) = S(t) + (\mu_1 - \mu_2)\tau + \sigma(W(T)-W(t)) + \sum_{k=1}^{N_1(\tau)} Y_{1,k} - \sum_{j=1}^{N_2(\tau)} Y_{2,j}

Note that the drift is now (\mu_1 - \mu_2)\tau, not \mu\tau: the expected jump contributions have been absorbed into the raw jump sums, which carry their own means. ∎

Proof of Proposition: Exact Price

From the Lemma, the terminal ability difference is:

S(T) = S(t) + (\mu_1 - \mu_2)\tau + \sigma(W(T)-W(t)) + \sum_{k=1}^{N_1(\tau)} Y_{1,k} - \sum_{j=1}^{N_2(\tau)} Y_{2,j}

where N_i(\tau) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_i\tau) are independent and Y_{1,k} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_{J_1}, \sigma_{J_1}^2), Y_{2,j} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_{J_2}, \sigma_{J_2}^2) are i.i.d. jump sizes.

Conditioning on (N_1=n_1, N_2=n_2). The number of jumps from each party is random, which prevents S(T)|\mathcal{F}_t from being Gaussian. We condition on (N_1=n_1, N_2=n_2), fixing the number of jump sizes. Conditional on these counts, the remaining random components are:

  • the Brownian increment: \sigma(W(T)-W(t)) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2\tau)
  • the n_1 independent party-one jump sizes: Y_{1,k} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_{J_1}, \sigma_{J_1}^2) for k=1,\ldots,n_1
  • the n_2 independent party-two jump sizes: Y_{2,j} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_{J_2}, \sigma_{J_2}^2) for j=1,\ldots,n_2

Since a sum of independent Gaussian random variables is Gaussian:

S(T) \mid \mathcal{F}_t, N_1=n_1, N_2=n_2 \sim \mathcal{N}(m_{n_1,n_2}, v_{n_1,n_2})

where

\begin{aligned} m_{n_1,n_2} &= S(t) + (\mu_1-\mu_2)\tau + n_1\mu_{J_1} - n_2\mu_{J_2} \\ v_{n_1,n_2} &= \sigma^2\tau + n_1\sigma_{J_1}^2 + n_2\sigma_{J_2}^2 \end{aligned}

Standardizing. Since the conditional distribution is Gaussian:

\mathbb{P}(S(T)>0|\mathcal{F}_t, N_1=n_1, N_2=n_2) = \Phi(Z_{n_1,n_2})

where Z_{n_1,n_2} = \frac{m_{n_1,n_2}}{\sqrt{v_{n_1,n_2}}} and \Phi is the standard normal CDF.

Averaging over all jump count pairs. By the law of total probability:

\pi(t) = \sum_{n_1=0}^{\infty} \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} p(n_1;\lambda_1\tau) \, p(n_2;\lambda_2\tau) \, \Phi(Z_{n_1,n_2})

where p(n;\lambda) = \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^n}{n!} is the Poisson probability mass function. ∎

Proof of Proposition: Exact Greeks

We differentiate the exact price formula with respect to S(t). Recall:

\pi(t) = \sum_{n_1=0}^{\infty} \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} p(n_1;\lambda_1\tau) \, p(n_2;\lambda_2\tau) \, \Phi(Z_{n_1,n_2})

where Z_{n_1,n_2} = \frac{S(t) + (\mu_1-\mu_2)\tau + n_1\mu_{J_1} - n_2\mu_{J_2}}{\sqrt{\sigma^2\tau + n_1\sigma_{J_1}^2 + n_2\sigma_{J_2}^2}}.

Delta (First Derivative):

\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial S} = \sum_{n_1=0}^{\infty} \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} p(n_1;\lambda_1\tau) \, p(n_2;\lambda_2\tau) \, \frac{\varphi(Z_{n_1,n_2})}{\sqrt{v_{n_1,n_2}}}

where \varphi(z) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-z^2/2} is the standard normal PDF and v_{n_1,n_2} = \sigma^2\tau + n_1\sigma_{J_1}^2 + n_2\sigma_{J_2}^2.

Gamma (Second Derivative):

\frac{\partial^2 \pi}{\partial S^2} = \sum_{n_1=0}^{\infty} \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} p(n_1;\lambda_1\tau) \, p(n_2;\lambda_2\tau) \, \frac{-Z_{n_1,n_2} \varphi(Z_{n_1,n_2})}{v_{n_1,n_2}}

These follow from differentiating \Phi(z), which yields \varphi(z), and applying the chain rule. ∎

Proof of Proposition: Exact Volatility

We apply Itô's formula for jump-diffusion processes to \pi(t) = \pi(t, S(t)):

d\pi = \frac{\partial \pi}{\partial t} dt + \frac{\partial \pi}{\partial S} dS + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 \pi}{\partial S^2} (dS)^2 + \sum_{j} [\pi(\cdot+Y_j)-\pi(\cdot)] dN_j

Continuous component: From the diffusion part of dS, the quadratic variation is (dS)^2 = \sigma^2 dt (to first order), yielding a continuous volatility contribution:

\Sigma_d^2 dt = \left(\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial S}\right)^2 \sigma^2 dt

Jump component: The two Poisson terms (party 1 and party 2 jumps) contribute:

\Sigma_J^2 dt = \lambda_1 E\left[(\pi(S+Y_1)-\pi(S))^2\right] dt + \lambda_2 E\left[(\pi(S-Y_2)-\pi(S))^2\right] dt

Total Volatility: Combining both contributions and taking square roots:

\Sigma_{\text{exact}}(t) = \sqrt{\left(\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial S}\right)^2 \sigma^2 + \lambda_1 E[(\pi(S+Y_1)-\pi(S))^2] + \lambda_2 E[(\pi(S-Y_2)-\pi(S))^2]}

∎

Proof of Proposition: Gaussian Price

Under the Gaussian approximation, we treat the total volatility as deterministic and use the compensated form from Appendix C. The terminal difference becomes:

S(T) = S(t) + \mu \tau + \sigma_{\text{total}} (W(T)-W(t))

where

\begin{aligned} \mu &= (\mu_1 - \mu_2 + \lambda_1 \mu_{J_1} - \lambda_2 \mu_{J_2}) \\ \sigma_{\text{total}}^2 &= \sigma^2 + \lambda_1 \sigma_{J_1}^2 + \lambda_2 \sigma_{J_2}^2 \end{aligned}

Conditional on \mathcal{F}_t, we have S(T) \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu', (\sigma_{\text{total}})^2\tau) where \mu' = S(t) + \mu\tau. Standardizing:

Z = \frac{S(T) - 0}{\sigma_{\text{total}}\sqrt{\tau}} = \frac{S(t) + \mu\tau}{\sigma_{\text{total}}\sqrt{\tau}}

Therefore:

\pi(t) = \mathbb{P}(S(T) > 0 \mid \mathcal{F}_t) = \Phi(Z) = \Phi\left(\frac{S(t) + \mu\tau}{\sigma_{\text{total}}\sqrt{\tau}}\right)

∎

Proof of Proposition: Gaussian Greeks

We differentiate \pi(t) = \Phi(Z) where Z = \frac{S(t) + \mu\tau}{\sigma_{\text{total}}\sqrt{\tau}}. Using the key relationships:

  • \frac{d\Phi}{dZ} = \varphi(Z)
  • \frac{d\varphi}{dZ} = -Z\varphi(Z)

Delta: Using the chain rule,

\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial S} = \frac{d\Phi}{dZ} \cdot \frac{\partial Z}{\partial S} = \varphi(Z) \cdot \frac{1}{\sigma_{\text{total}}\sqrt{\tau}}

Gamma: Differentiating again,

\frac{\partial^2 \pi}{\partial S^2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial S}\left[\varphi(Z) \cdot \frac{1}{\sigma_{\text{total}}\sqrt{\tau}}\right] = \frac{d\varphi}{dZ} \cdot \left(\frac{1}{\sigma_{\text{total}}\sqrt{\tau}}\right)^2 = \frac{-Z\varphi(Z)}{\sigma_{\text{total}}^2 \tau}

Theta: We differentiate with respect to time t. Note that \tau = T - t, so \frac{\partial}{\partial t} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial \tau}. The quotient rule and chain rule give:

\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial t} = \varphi(Z) \left[\frac{\mu}{Z\sigma_{\text{total}}\sqrt{\tau}} - \frac{Z}{2\tau}\right]

∎

Proof of Proposition: Drift is Zero (Martingale Property)

Under risk-neutral valuation, the price process \pi(t, S(t)) must be a martingale. We apply Itô's formula to verify this.

d\pi = \left(\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial t} + \mu \frac{\partial \pi}{\partial S} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\frac{\partial^2 \pi}{\partial S^2}\right) dt + \sigma \frac{\partial \pi}{\partial S} dW + \text{Jump terms}

Continuous component drift: The drift term is the bracket in parentheses above. For a martingale, we require:

\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial t} + \mu \frac{\partial \pi}{\partial S} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\frac{\partial^2 \pi}{\partial S^2} = 0

Jump component drift: The expected drift from party i jumps is:

\mathbb{E}[\pi(S+Y_i) - \pi(S)] \, \lambda_i dt

Under the risk-neutral measure, this is also zero on average (jump risk premium is priced into the initial condition). Combining continuous and jump components, the total drift of d\pi is zero, confirming that \pi(t) is a martingale. ∎

Proof of Proposition: Gaussian Volatility

In the Gaussian approximation, \pi(t) = \Phi(Z(t)) where Z(t) = \frac{S(t) + \mu\tau}{\sigma_{\text{total}}\sqrt{\tau}}. Applying Itô's formula:

d\pi = \varphi(Z) \, dZ + \text{higher order terms}

The dynamics of Z are driven by dS (the Brownian and jump components). The diffusion volatility of Z is:

\Sigma_d = \frac{\sigma}{\sigma_{\text{total}}} \cdot \frac{\varphi(Z)}{\sqrt{\tau}}

The jump volatility contributions from both parties sum to:

\Sigma_J^2 = (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2) \sigma_J^2 \cdot \left(\frac{\varphi(Z)}{\sigma_{\text{total}}\sqrt{\tau}}\right)^2

where \sigma_J^2 is an effective jump variance. Combining both contributions:

\Sigma_{\text{total}}^2 = \varphi(Z)^2 \left[\frac{\sigma^2 + (\lambda_1+\lambda_2)\sigma_J^2}{\sigma_{\text{total}}^2}\right] \cdot \frac{1}{\tau}

Since the bracketed term equals 1 under our normalization, we obtain:

\Sigma_{\text{total}}(t) = \frac{\varphi(\Phi^{-1}(\pi(t)))}{\sqrt{T-t}}

This shows that volatility is maximized when \pi(t) = 0.5 (i.e., Z=0) and decreases toward the boundaries as the probability approaches 0 or 1. ∎

Appendix A: Compensated Poisson Process

The raw jump process J_i(t) = \sum_{k=1}^{N_i(t)} Y_{i,k} where N_i(t) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_i t) has expectation \mathbb{E}[J_i(t)] = \lambda_i t \, \mu_{J_i}. Thus, J_i(t) is not a martingale (it has drift \lambda_i \mu_{J_i}).

To isolate the martingale (unpredictable) component, we subtract the compensator:

\tilde{J}_i(t) = J_i(t) - \lambda_i \mu_{J_i} t

Now, \mathbb{E}[\tilde{J}_i(t)] = \mathbb{E}[J_i(t)] - \lambda_i \mu_{J_i} t = 0, so \tilde{J}_i(t) is a martingale. This allows us to decompose the dynamics into predictable drift (which may be priced under risk-neutral measure) and unpredictable martingale noise. ∎

Appendix B: Combined Brownian Motion

Given two correlated Brownian motions W_1, W_2 with correlation \rho_{12}, we form the linear combination:

U = \sigma_1 W_1 - \sigma_2 W_2

The variance of U is:

\operatorname{Var}[U] = \sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 - 2\rho_{12}\sigma_1\sigma_2

Define \sigma = \sqrt{\operatorname{Var}[U]} and the normalized process:

W = \frac{U}{\sigma} = \frac{1}{\sigma}(\sigma_1 W_1 - \sigma_2 W_2)

Then W is a standard Brownian motion (variance 1) and:

\sigma_1 W_1 - \sigma_2 W_2 = \sigma W

This shows that the combined diffusion in S(t) can be written in terms of a single Brownian motion W with coefficient \sigma. ∎

Appendix C: Variance of Compensated Jump Process

For a single compensated jump process, \tilde{J}_i(t) = J_i(t) - \lambda_i \mu_{J_i} t. The variance is:

\operatorname{Var}[\tilde{J}_i(T) - \tilde{J}_i(t)] = E\left[\left(\sum_{k=1}^{N_i(\tau)} Y_{i,k} - \lambda_i\mu_{J_i}\tau\right)^2\right]

Expanding and using the properties of the Poisson process:

= E\left[\left(\sum_{k=1}^{N_i(\tau)} Y_{i,k}\right)^2\right] - (E[\sum Y_{i,k}])^2 = \operatorname{Var}\left[\sum_{k=1}^{N_i(\tau)} Y_{i,k}\right]

By the law of total variance:

\operatorname{Var}\left[\sum Y_{i,k}\right] = E[N_i(\tau)] \operatorname{Var}[Y_i] + \operatorname{Var}[N_i(\tau)] (E[Y_i])^2 = \lambda_i\tau(\sigma_{J_i}^2 + \mu_{J_i}^2)

Therefore:

\operatorname{Var}[\tilde{J}_i(\tau)] = \lambda_i\tau(\sigma_{J_i}^2 + \mu_{J_i}^2)

For the difference \tilde{J}(t) = \tilde{J}_1(t) - \tilde{J}_2(t), using independence of the two Poisson streams:

\operatorname{Var}[\tilde{J}(\tau)] = \lambda_1\tau(\sigma_{J_1}^2 + \mu_{J_1}^2) + \lambda_2\tau(\sigma_{J_2}^2 + \mu_{J_2}^2)

∎

Appendix D: Explicit Jump Count Examples

The exact price formula sums over all possible jump count pairs (n_1, n_2). Below are the first several terms:

\begin{aligned} \pi(t) &= p(0;\lambda_1\tau)p(0;\lambda_2\tau) \Phi(Z_{0,0}) \\[6pt] &\quad + p(1;\lambda_1\tau)p(0;\lambda_2\tau) \Phi(Z_{1,0}) \\[6pt] &\quad + p(0;\lambda_1\tau)p(1;\lambda_2\tau) \Phi(Z_{0,1}) \\[6pt] &\quad + p(1;\lambda_1\tau)p(1;\lambda_2\tau) \Phi(Z_{1,1}) \\[6pt] &\quad + p(2;\lambda_1\tau)p(0;\lambda_2\tau) \Phi(Z_{2,0}) \\[6pt] &\quad + \cdots \end{aligned}

where each term weights the conditional price \Phi(Z_{n_1,n_2}) by the probability of observing exactly n_1 party-1 jumps and n_2 party-2 jumps. The standardized terminal difference for each pair is:

Z_{n_1,n_2} = \frac{S(t) + (\mu_1-\mu_2)\tau + n_1\mu_{J_1} - n_2\mu_{J_2}}{\sqrt{\sigma^2\tau + n_1\sigma_{J_1}^2 + n_2\sigma_{J_2}^2}}

For a practical implementation, the sum is truncated at some maximum number of jumps (e.g., N_{\max} = 20) since the Poisson probabilities decay rapidly for large n. ∎
